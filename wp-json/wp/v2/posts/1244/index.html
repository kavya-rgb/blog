I’ve been a member of this subreddit for quite a while, and I’ll often reply to threads in my spare time. Over the last couple of months, I’ve noticed a bashing of Fiverr, and upWork in how they’re trash for developers. And these comments are partially right. For the average developer, you’ll get out quoted by sellers from third world countries, and finding work will be a real struggle. You have no reviews, yet you want to charge a decent amount – which is fair. Everyone should be paid for what they’re worth. That’s why you try not to be an average developer. <\/p>\n

My story with Fiverr started a number of years ago. At the time, I had very basic experience with WordPress, and I was hired by a friend to build them a website for their new shop. Sure I only got paid \u00a3200, but at the time, it was more than enough, as I learnt that I really enjoyed working with websites. Said friend was buying graphics off Fiverr, and asked me why I don’t offer my services on Fiverr. At the time, he thought I was a really experienced developer \\~ I was not. He was under this impression since I was always the tech guy people would come to. <\/p>\n

So, I signed up to Fiverr offering bug fixes for wordpress. It was mostly basic stuff like plugins not working, theme errors etc. I was basically uploading\/changing files and getting paid. Getting paid $5. Yes $5 is what I charged. Why you ask? Because I had no reviews, and I knew reviews were key to success on this platform. It took months, of maybe two reviews a week, to build up a profile of around 40-50 reviews. I then upped my price to $15. Sales actually started to increase when I upped my price. <\/p>\n

Eventually after 100 reviews, I started charging $25\/hour. I would price up a job on how long I would think it would take, and do it. If it went over, I wouldn’t ask for me. It’s my fault for not accurately predicting how long it would take. Clients really liked this, and would often return.<\/p>\n

Despite everything I’ve said above, I do believe my success came from how I setup my profile. I clearly mention I’m from the UK, and a small intro about how I got into development, and how I don’t just do it because it’s a job, but because I enjoy it. This seems to resonate with people. They seem to want to have an experience with me, than a cheaper worker in another country because we’re able to connect on a level more than just a job, and they don’t want the risk of the brief not being understood, and then having to find another seller. 60% of my buyers are in the UK.<\/p>\n

When applying for jobs in the buyer requests section, I won’t post a generic reply. I will always start it with “Hey there, I’m Josh. I’m a developer from the UK” – and then I’ll go into details on how I will solve their job. I’ve received so many jobs by doing this. But it gets to a point that I don’t have to do buyer requests because I have so much work on, that I don’t need any more. <\/p>\n

At this point, I’ve been working full time on Fiverr for around 2 years. Making good income. I enjoy the freedom of being able to work when I want, how many hours I want. And a client who I have done some wordpress work for, offers me a full-time job in his company of 100+ employees, with a salary 1.5 of the area in my area. The work is with a language I have minimal experience with, and it would take me a good while to get up to date – yet the client who offered me this job says this is fine. And I totally believe this is down the relationship I built with them. <\/p>\n

So now I’m leaving my work at Fiverr, and switching to working remote for a company full time, and it feels great. It feels great knowing I won’t have to work with WordPress again. Sure there is a lot of money with wordpress, but it’s shite having to deal with websites that weren’t built by developers, but by normal people dragging and dropping. Using plugins for basic stuff. I don’t think I ever want to return to working with WordPress.<\/p>\n\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
